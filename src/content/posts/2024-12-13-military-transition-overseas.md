##  Military Transition Overseas  

I was stationed in Germany for roughly six years of my military career. It comes as no surprise that I would meet my wife here and only a little odd that I would decide to settle down in Germany after completing my service.

With that decision came the unique challenge of transitioning out of the military while overseas and into the local economy.

The process, with all its nuances, could fill a book. We won't get to everything in this post, but hopefully this gets you in the right direction.

***Note: I served in the United States Army so my experience may not be 1:1 transferable to other branches. However, the general concepts hold true.***

---

## TLDR

Here's a starting point timeline for those that don't want to read / re-read the whole article.

- Figure out your remaining time obligation
- Talk with your command about your tentative SkillBridge timeline
- Decide how much terminal leave you want to take then plan out your remaining leave
- Research the immigration requirements for the country where you want to transition 
- Start learning the language now
- Consider any certifications you want to get before becoming a civilian
- If you're attending school, pay attention to admission deadlines and entry exam requirements
- ***Make your master timeline with all the dates from above.***

##  Overseas Time Obligation

There are some differences in timeline when deciding to leave the military while stationed overseas.

**The main one is that you probably need to complete your overseas tour commitment.**

Serving overseas usually comes with a time requirement to complete the tour in its entirety. This can be waived by the higher military powers, but usually not for ETS reasons.

I remember my friends leaving the military within 3-6 months of them making their decision. It was closer to a 2-3 year plan for me.

I recommend diving into the regulation (AR 614-30 for the Army folks) and talking with your human resources people early on.

## Research Immigration Requirements

If you're looking to live overseas as a "normal person," the military transition is only one piece of the pie.

In addition to that, you have the wonderful task of figuring out immigration processes in your target country.

There are too many differences to list specifics by country, but here's a general list of things to consider:

- Do you need a visa to enter the country?
- What residency permit are you applying for?
- What are the potential tax implications?
  - Keep in mind, Americans have to file taxes worldwide. You'll generally pay no US taxes, unless you're raking in the big bucks, but the reporting is still required.
- How does health insurance work?
- How do you get a driver's license?
- Are there any official language requirements for residency?
